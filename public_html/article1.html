Study Tips Backed by Science

17 August Constantin Lisson Studying,

1 Location, Location, Location

Change it up! Spending hours and hours in the library may ease your conscience (and subdue your parents) but studies suggest that changing your studying environment can help you retain information more effectively. Your brain tends to associate the same material with different locales, forcing the memory to strengthen with a variety of different associations.

2 Narrate It

Reading aloud helps your brain learn in two different ways simultaneously – visually and auditorily. Make the content more memorable by coming up with funny ways to explain the concepts, in the form of a story, poem, or even a song. [Note: this may get you funny looks in a public place, and almost certainly guarantee getting angrily shushed in the library. You’ve been warned.]

3 Pull a Pavlov

Study in little chunks, and work towards a break or a treat (both are essential, so try to alternate or mix it up). If you complete a quiz, for example, have a cookie or cup of coffee as a reward and to help form positive associations with the process. Studying does not have to be a gruelling, painful process – make it fun and do other activities between studying to break the monotony and to stay motivated.

4 Go Unplugged

While some material and resources necessary for studying will be on your laptop or online, there’s no doubt that the internet can be a major source of distraction whilst studying. Put your phone far away (this means in another room, or at least out of sight and on silent in your bag) and download an app that blocks time-wasting sites. You’ll be amazed at how much more you get done.

5 Pen & Paper

Studies have shown that we don’t retain as much information when reading directly from a screen, (such as your laptop or tablet) and that writing is more effective than typing for studying purposes. Kick it old school and write out your notes – make summaries, mind-maps and whatever else works for you. Whip out highlighters and colourful pens to help your notes make a lasting impression.

6 Embrace Your Inner Jock

The ‘dumb jock’ stereotype is a myth: Exercise is an important tool in helping you retain information. Work at least half an hour of cardiovascular exercise into your day, and your brain will thank you. Studies have shown that exercise helps improve our processing ability, among other important cognitive functions. It’ll also help stabilise your mood by producing those wonderful ‘happy hormones’ (endorphins), helping keep the study blues at bay.

7 Sleepy Time

This is a two-part tip. Firstly, get enough sleep – which means at least seven to eight hours. This may seem obvious, but with the tendency to cram, many students miss out on this essential rest. Lack of sleep can cause one to be more prone to stress and to perform poorly in tests, so avoid cramming at all costs. Secondly, studies have shown that the brain strengthens new memories during sleep, so try do a little reading or note-making just before you pass out. [Note: don’t bring your study material to bed – this prevents you from being able to shut off, hindering relaxation and causing unnecessary stress].

8 Get Quizzical

Testing yourself is an old yet highly-effective study method. If you find yourself panicked during the prep-quiz, don’t worry – that’s part of the process. In fact, if you struggle to produce certain answers, you’ll be more likely to remember them in the actual test after having obsessed over them beforehand.

9 Sip on Somethin’

Stay hydrated to avoid mind-numbing headaches and fatigue, and allow yourself a moderate amount of something caffeinated. Studies have shown that (although not recommended in excess) coffee and tea can help keep us alert and also motivate us to keep going.

10 Experiment

Ideally, one shouldn’t start experimenting with different study methods days before the test or exam in question. If you begin studying far enough in advance, take the time to see what works best for you. Are you an early bird, or a night owl? Are you a lone wolf or a pack-dog? Try out a few group study sessions to see if that works for you, and alternate with solo-stints at home, the library, or wherever you’re able to focus. Soon you’ll figure out how, when and where you learn best.
